# Tidal Metabolism

## Algorithmic Philosophy

The ocean does not flow — it breathes. Every six hours, the coastline inhales and exhales through tidal cycles that have persisted since the moon first locked into orbit. This philosophy treats the Korean Strait as a living membrane: a boundary layer where warm Tsushima currents collide with cold coastal upwelling, where dissolved salts trace invisible rivers through the water column, where wind stress carves ephemeral topographies on the surface. The algorithm models this membrane not as a map, but as a metabolic system — data points are organs, currents are circulatory pathways, and temperature differentials are the thermal signatures of a body in perpetual homeostasis.

The computational core operates through three interlocked systems, each running at a different temporal resolution to create depth through temporal parallax. The first system — tidal oscillation — drives slow, massive wave functions across the canvas, their amplitudes drawn directly from centimeter-level tide gauge readings at Busan, Ulsan, Tongyeong, and Geoje. These are not decorative sine waves; they are the literal mathematical representation of gravitational pull between celestial bodies, transcribed into pixel displacement. The second system — thermal diffusion — treats each observation station as a heat source, radiating color gradients that blend and interfere according to actual temperature differentials measured in real-time. The third system — aeolian transport — releases thousands of massless particles into vector fields constructed from wind direction and speed data, each particle's trajectory a ghost-trace of atmospheric force made visible.

The meticulously crafted algorithm refuses the temptation of spectacle. Where lesser implementations would amplify data into dramatic visualizations, this system operates through radical restraint — each data point influences the visual field at precisely the scale it operates in reality. A 3.6 m/s wind does not create a hurricane on screen; it nudges particles with exactly proportional force. A 0.5°C temperature differential does not produce a color explosion; it shifts hue by a barely perceptible degree. The result is an image that rewards sustained attention: what initially appears as a dark, nearly monochrome field gradually reveals itself as a living system of extraordinary subtlety, where every flicker and drift corresponds to an actual physical measurement happening right now in the waters between Korea and Japan.

The conceptual seed is drawn from the Korean notion of 물질 (muljil) — literally "material quality" but also the Jeju haenyeo term for diving into the sea. The algorithm embodies this double meaning: it is simultaneously a visualization of material flows (water, heat, wind) and an act of immersion into data as deep as the ocean itself. The particle system references the haenyeo's breath-hold pattern — particles are born at the surface, dive into the field, and must eventually return to reset. This metabolic cycle of descent and return, of data-gathering and surface-breaking, gives the visualization its distinctive pulse: not the mechanical tick of a clock, but the organic rhythm of lungs working against pressure.

The palette is derived not from artistic preference but from oceanographic convention: cold blues for temperatures below 12°C graduating through teals and sage greens to warm ambers above 16°C, with salinity encoded as opacity — higher salt content produces denser, more opaque visual matter. Wind traces are rendered in a spectral cyan that exists at the threshold of visibility, appearing only when attention settles into the image. The master-level implementation ensures that every parameter has been tuned through painstaking iteration: noise octaves calibrated to produce organic turbulence without chaos, particle lifespans balanced to create density without clutter, color transitions smoothed to prevent banding while preserving data fidelity.
